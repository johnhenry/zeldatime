# A Natural History of Hyrule: Species Across the Timeline

*A companion codex piece. Unlike this site's two core papers, this one was compiled from the games themselves and standard series reference material (including* Hyrule Historia*'s developer-written lore sections), not run through the adversarial verification pipeline — species lore is well-documented common knowledge, but treat fine details as fan-reference-grade rather than independently verified. Each species links to its interactive [thread](/threads), where you can trace it game by game.*

## Why species are a timeline instrument

Nintendo rarely dates anything in Zelda. What it does instead — consistently, deliberately — is *evolve its peoples*. When a species changes form between games, the change is the timestamp: it tells you an age has passed, a cataclysm intervened, a branch diverged. The fandom's deepest timeline arguments are, at bottom, arguments about biology.

## The flood's children: Zora → Rito, Kokiri → Korok

![The Wind Waker box art](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/79/The_Legend_of_Zelda_The_Wind_Waker.jpg)
*The Wind Waker (2002) — debut of the Rito, framed by Hyrule Historia as flood-evolved Zora.*

The Adult Timeline's Great Flood is the series' most explicit evolutionary event. *Hyrule Historia* frames The Wind Waker's bird-like **Rito** as descendants of the aquatic **Zora** — an ocean-covered world having, paradoxically, pushed its water people into the sky. The same flood turns the **Kokiri**, Ocarina of Time's eternal forest children, into the **Koroks**: walking leaf-spirits who migrate with the Great Deku Tree and no longer resemble anything human.

![Breath of the Wild box art](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c6/The_Legend_of_Zelda_Breath_of_the_Wild.jpg)
*Breath of the Wild (2017) — puts Zora and Rito on screen simultaneously, no comment offered.*

Both transformations made in-fiction sense *as long as they stayed on the Adult branch*. Then Breath of the Wild — deliberately unplaced on any branch — put Zora and Rito on screen **simultaneously**, in the same Hyrule, with no comment. If the Rito evolved *from* the Zora, their coexistence is a paradox on two of the three branches. Community readings split three ways: BOTW descends from a merged or convergent future where both lineages exist; the BOTW Rito are an unrelated species with a reused name; or — the reading this site finds most consistent with Nintendo's stated posture — the contradiction is intentional ambiguity, one more removed answer. The [Zora & Rito thread](/threads/zora-and-rito) traces every appearance.

## The constants: Gorons, Gerudo, Sheikah

![Ocarina of Time box art](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/57/The_Legend_of_Zelda_Ocarina_of_Time.jpg)
*Ocarina of Time (1998) — debut of the Gorons, Gerudo, and the Sheikah's shadow-tribe era.*

Against the flood's shapeshifters, three peoples are notable for *refusing* to change:

**Gorons** appear in more eras and branches than any other non-Hylian species — mountains full of them in Ocarina of Time, merchants wandering the Great Sea despite the flood, a village in New Hyrule's Fire Realm, a mining boom under BOTW's Death Mountain. Their biological stability across every branch makes them the timeline's control group: wherever you are in history, a Goron looks like a Goron.

**Gerudo** carry the series' strangest reproductive rule — one male born each century — which doubles as narrative machinery: that male is Ganondorf, and the tribe's history is the history of dealing with what it keeps producing. The species persists visibly on the Child branch (Four Swords Adventures' reborn Ganondorf) and into the distant future (Gerudo Town, Riju), making them the timeline's most consequential bloodline after the royal family itself.

**Sheikah** evolve not biologically but *technologically* — from Ocarina of Time's shadow-tribe ninjas to the ancient-tech civilization whose towers, shrines, and Slate define the entire BOTW era's aesthetic. If the Gorons are the timeline's biological constant, the Sheikah are its rate of technological change — and the in-fiction ancestors of this very site's design language.

## Reading the menagerie

The same dating logic extends past the great races. **Cuccos** have kept their revenge-flock behavior intact from A Link to the Past through Breath of the Wild — a forty-year-old joke maintained with more consistency than most of the series' lore. **Lynels** vanished for three decades after the NES era and returned in BOTW as apex predators, a deliberate deep-cut resurrection. **Octoroks** and **Like Likes** simply never left. When Nintendo wants an era to feel ancient, it reaches for the original 1986 bestiary; when it wants continuity, it hands you a Cucco.
